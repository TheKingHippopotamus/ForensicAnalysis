01 Executive Summary ▼

NIKE, Inc. is undergoing a pronounced cyclical contraction. FY2025 represents the worst operational year in more than half a decade: revenue fell 9.8% to $46.3B, net income cratered 43.5% to $3.2B, and operating cash flow was cut in half to $3.7B. The deterioration is broad-based across every geographic segment and Converse, with no obvious haven.

Forensic screening surfaces a mixed but increasingly cautious picture. The Beneish M-Score of -2.46 sits just below the manipulation threshold of -2.22 — close enough to warrant monitoring. The Altman Z-Score has slipped into the grey zone at 2.33, a meaningful downgrade from the safe zone the company occupied in prior years. Retained earnings have turned negative (-$727M), a landmark deterioration for a blue-chip franchise.

Working capital metrics flash yellow: DSO rose 18% even as revenues declined (a classic channel-stuffing signal), DPO surged 31% (supplier stretching), and the inventory reserve jumped 50%. Combined with a 64% increase in prepaid demand creation costs against falling sales, the data suggests management is investing aggressively to reverse the decline, but results have yet to follow.

Bottom Line: NIKE remains profitable and cash-generative at a level far above insolvency risk, but the velocity and breadth of its financial deterioration are uncharacteristic for this franchise. The clustering of forensic indicators near threshold values suggests elevated — though not yet critical — risk. This company needs a visible inflection within the next 2-3 quarters to avoid further score degradation.

11 Methodology ▼

This report applies a battery of established forensic and quantitative models to NIKE's SEC filings. Each model targets a distinct risk dimension. All data is sourced from NIKE's 10-K and 10-Q filings as reported to the SEC via XBRL.

Beneish M-Score

Eight-variable model developed by Professor Messod Beneish (1999) to detect earnings manipulation. Combines receivables, margins, asset quality, sales growth, depreciation, SGA, accruals, and leverage indices. Scores above -2.22 indicate elevated manipulation probability.

Altman Z-Score

Five-ratio composite developed by Edward Altman (1968) to predict bankruptcy probability within two years. Incorporates working capital, retained earnings, EBIT, market equity, and revenue efficiency. Below 1.81 = distress; above 2.99 = safe.

Piotroski F-Score

Nine-point binary scoring system by Joseph Piotroski (2000) assessing fundamental strength across profitability (4 tests), leverage/liquidity (3 tests), and operating efficiency (2 tests). Scores 0-3 = weak; 7-9 = strong.

Sloan Accrual Ratio

Measures the proportion of earnings derived from accruals versus cash. Developed by Richard Sloan (1996). High accrual ratios (>±10%) suggest lower earnings persistence and potential manipulation.

Cash Conversion Analysis

Measures the ratio of operating cash flow to net income. Persistent ratios below 1.0x indicate that reported earnings are not being converted to cash — a quality-of-earnings red flag.

Working Capital Forensics

Tracks DSO, DIO, DPO, and the cash conversion cycle for signs of channel stuffing (rising DSO), inventory buildup (rising DIO), or supplier stretching (rising DPO). Cross-referenced with revenue trends for context.
